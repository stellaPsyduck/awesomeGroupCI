\section{Introduction}
Forecasting of financial time series is a significant challenge in computational intelligence
due to the high volatility, non-linearity and inconsistent variety of global markets. Traditional 
univariate models often try to predict stock price movements in isolation, not accounting
for the deep rooted dependencies and shared behaviours between firms in the same sector. 
This project investigates Multi-Task Learning, a form of inductive transfer
to improve predictive accuracy by explicity modeling these interconnections. 

By utilizing shared representations, the Multi-Task Learning framework forces the model to favor
parameters that generalize across different but associated market behaviours. This approach leverages 
sector-wide knowledge to improve the performance of individual target tasks. Our study focuses on 
a diverse cohort of 25 companies spanning the hardware and software sectors, derived from the 
iShares S\&P/TSX Capped Information Technology Index and Vanguard Information Technology ETFs. 

The primary objective is to develop a robust architecture capable of capturing complex, non-linear 
dependencies in financial data from 2009 to 2017. To evaluate the efficacy of the Multi-Task Learning 
approach, we compare two sophisticated architectures:
\begin{itemize}
\item Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Networks: A recurrent architecture designed for sequential data, 
    implemented with a shared hidden layers and task-specific output heads.
\item Multi-Task Extra Trees: An ensemble method utilizing multi-output regression to optimize node 
    splits across all tasks simultaneously.
\end{itemize}
By comparing these multi-tasking frameworks against traditional single-prediction baselines, 
this study demonstrates how shared representations can reduce variance and provide more stable 
price forecasts in volatile financial environments.

\subsection*{Objectives}
The primary objective of this research is to evaluate whether a multi-task learning framework 
can improve the predictive accuracy of individual stock prices by leveraging historical information 
from correlated stocks within the same sector. To achieve this, the project also contains the following 
sub-objectives:
\begin{itemize}
\item Comparative Performance Analysis: To perform a good comparison between multi-task learning architectures 
    and traditional single-task (univariate) baselines to determine if modeling stocks in isolation fails 
    to capture critical sector-wide dependencies.
\item Validation of Inductive Transfer: To assess the effectiveness of inductive transfer, specifically 
    the ability to improve the performance of a target task after learning to model related tasks within 
    the technology sector.
\item Evaluation of Inductive Bias: To determine if forcing the model to favour parameters that are 
    effective across multiple target tasks leads to more stable forecasts in highly volatile financial 
    environments.
\item Cross-Model Generalization: To apply the multi-task learning approach across 2 distinct computational 
    intelligence architectures, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Multi-Task Extra Trees to ensure 
    the findings are robust across different algorithmic types.
\item Sector Interrelationship Testing: To validate if the shared representation between hardware and software 
    firms provides a accuracy boost by modeling their interconnections rather than modeling them in 
    isolation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}
\subsubsection*{Data Availability}
Given the abundance of detailed historical stock market data, the dataset was carefully chosen.
A mix of hardware and software companies between 2009 and 2017 was selected to ensure that the
data exhibits behaviours uninfluenced by external severe market-changing events. Training on 
this data allowed for the architecture to capture the complex, non-linear market behaviors that
traditional models struggle to represent. The timeframe was specifically restricted to the period
from 2009 through 2017 to avoid two major economic anomalies: the 2008 financial crash and the 
COVID-19 pandemic. Excluding these catastrophic events prevents the model from unintentionally
being trained on massive market disruptions.

\subsubsection*{Datasets}
The specific companies in the dataset were taken from the iShares S\&P/TSX Capped Information
Technology Index ETF and the Vanguard Information Technology ETF. These professionally curated
funds were used to ensure a baseline of market viability and relevance. To guarantee sufficient
training data, a total of 25 companies were selected: 5 from iShare and 20 from the Vanguard ETF.
The selected companies were subsequently divided into three categories based on their market
capitalization: large-cap (greater than \$120,000,000 USD), mid-cap (between \$120,000,000 and
\$50,000,000 USD), and small-cap (less than \$50,000,000 USD).

\subsubsection*{Intra-Sector Relationships and Multitasking}
A varied composition of technology companies, spanning across a large range of valuations,
was deliberately selected to optimize the architecture for multi-task learning. This introduced
the models to complex intra-sector relationships, allowing them to learn how behaviours in one
sector influence or correlate with another. By multi-tasking across these datasets, the model
avoids overfitting to a single company size and develops a stronger understanding of the underlying
patterns of the market.

\subsubsection*{Multi-Tasking Extra Trees (or Extremely Randomized Trees)}
Extra Trees (ET) is a tree ensemble method like Random Forests, but it produces more decorrelated trees. It uses
 the entire data set to build decision trees, rather than bootstrapping as in random forests. It also differs
in its threshold selection, which randomly selects where to split the feature rather than using an optimization
method. It still chooses $m_{try}$ number of random features to choose the feature splits to compare, where
$m_{try} = \lfloor \frac{p}{3} \rfloor$ or $m_{try} = \lfloor \sqrt{p} \rfloor$ and p is the number of features. There are two relevant extensions to this algorithm that make it similar
to our LSTM hard parameter multi-tasking \cite{rfmulti}. \\
The first is introducing multi-output, which essentially means the loss function is the summation of sample error
and task outputs. This effectively makes each node split
consider the error of the candiate splits' effect across all tasks \cite{rfmulti}.
The second is introducing task-focused splits, or the ability to make decisions based on what the task is
being predicted. To do this, a random group of $m_{try}$ features and S random possible
subsets of tasks are selected (S$\epsilon \mathbb{Z} < T$ randomly selected). Each feature split is randomized,
and the multi-output error is calculated. Then, $f_k$ (Equation \ref{fk}) is calculated for each candidate task subset or feature;
this is the prediction. The error is calculated from the prediction and used to choose the best split at the node \cite{rfmulti}. 

\begin{equation}
    \label{fk}
    f_k = \frac{\sum_{\nu \epsilon I_k} y_{\nu} + \alpha \frac{1}{\lfloor I \rfloor} \sum_{w \epsilon I} y_w}{\lfloor I_k \rfloor + \alpha}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textit{``I denotes the set of sample indexes at the current node, $I_k$ the set of sample indexes of the task $k$
at the current node and $\alpha$ is a regularization parameter weighting the influence of the tasks with default
value 1. Then, the task cut point is randomly selected from the interval $(\min_k f_k, \max_k f_k)$" - Schmid et. al \cite{rfmulti}}

To implement this method, we used R, since there is a pre-made R package for multi-tasking tree enssemblers. It is technically archived in newer versions
of R, but can be downloaded \href{https://cran.r-project.org/src/contrib/Archive/extraTrees/}{here}. This was decided because the paper explaining the metholody
used this package \cite{rfmulti}.

\subsubsection*{Mean Absolute Error (Criterion and Metric)}
Mean Absolute Error (MAE) is a metric robust to outliers \cite{slater}. 
For that reason, the MAE was chosen as the loss function, and the models
were evaluated with this metric. The MAE could not be used as the “criterion”
or error to minimize in extra trees because the R package did not support it.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
\subsubsection*{Misaligned Dates}
Due to different market holidays, some days in the data included both null values and
recorded prices. The days with null values were removed to keep the dataset consistent.
Dropping a few days of data should not obscure the stocks’ growth patterns and relationships.
\subsubsection*{Logarithmic Scale}
The data was scaled logarithmically. This was to ensure stable predictions by reducing 
 effect of outliers on the model and to account for the exponential patterns in each stock's data.
The predictions become more stable because most analysis techniques assume additive data, including
machine learning.\cite{log_scale}. \\
The scaling affect is shown in appendix \ref{appendix_a}, the medium stock ACIW is on a similar scale to the large 
stock AAPL after logging the data.
\subsubsection*{Lag Length}
Lags are data from a previous date; for example, lag 1 at time $t$ is the data point at time $ t-1$ \cite{slater}.
This idea is from time series, where it is assumed that lags of the data have some affect on the current $t$'s data. \\
For this model, 60 lags were used because a quarter of the fiscal year is approximately this many days \cite{stockgro}. This value considers
only days when the markets are actually open.

\subsubsection*{LSTMs}
Pytorch requires LSTM inputs to be 3D tensors \cite{3d_tensor_lstm}. The data looks at stocks, with the only attribute 
being the stock price. Referring to figure \ref{lstm_diagram}, the matrix on the left would be an example 3D tensor input,
with each vector $\vec{x}$ representing all the stocks of interest at the given t. There is no third dimension depicted,
as the only attribute of each stock of interest is the given price at time t. \\
The times within the input are $\vec{x_{t,n}}$ and the lags of $\vec{x_{tn}}$, which represent our rolling window,
 since $\vec{x_{t+1}}$ should use all the previous lags for its prediction.
\subsubsection*{Extra Trees}
To turn the stock price data into an acceptable input for a tree ensemble, the lags must be added manually.
Each datapoint will then have 60 attributes representing the 60 associated lags
lags \cite{timeseries_data}. Days without all 60 previous lags have been dropped.

\subsubsection*{Extra Trees}
For Extra Trees, single-task and multi-task, the parameters that could be tuned  are \cite{rfmulti}:

\begin{itemize}
\item The number of grown trees
\item The cardinality of the sample of features at every node is $m_{try}$
\item The number of random split values at each node
\item The minimum number of observations that each terminal node should contain (stopping criteria)
\end{itemize}

The models in this paper are tuned for $m_{try}$, the number of random splits/cuts, and the number
of terminal node observations. The number of trees was set to the default of 500 \cite{extratreedoc}.
Any excess grown tree doesn't impact the predictive power of the model, and 500 is likely enough for
the tasks of interest \cite{toomanytree}.

\subsection{Multi-Task LSTM}
The Multi-Task LSTM architecture was designed to leverage shared temporal features 
across a synchronized dataset of 25 stocks. By utilizing shared hidden layers, the model 
implements an inductive bias that favours parameters generalizing across the technology sector.
\subsubsection*{Hyperparameters}
The optimal configurations for the MTL model was determined through a systematic 3 stage ablation study 
evaluating layer count, hidden dimension size, and learning rates. The final architecture selected for 
the 200-epoch evaluation run consisted of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LSTM Layers:} 2. As illustrated in Figure \ref{mtl_layers}, 2 layers provided the optimal balance between model capacity and generalization, whereas deeper architectures showed a sharp increase in validation error.
    \item \textbf{Hidden Dimension:} 128. The selection of 128 hidden units was justified by the performance trends in Figure \ref{mtl_hidden}, proving sufficient to capture high-dimensional cross-stock correlations without over-parameterization.
    \item \textbf{Learning Rate:} 0.0005. Figure \ref{mtl_lr} highlights that this rate allowed for the most stable descent into the convergence learning cliff, avoiding the instability found in more aggressive rates.
    \item \textbf{Dropout:} 0.2. Applied to the shared hidden layers to provide robust regularization against the high volatility of the technology sector.
    \item \textbf{Batch Size:} 64. Utilized to provide a stable gradient estimate for the weighted sum of task-specific loss functions while maintaining enough stochastic noise for effective generalization.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Algorithm Performances}
The multi-task LSTM achieved a final aggregate test Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of \textbf{0.4444} across all 25 task-specific output heads. 
As illustrated in Figure \ref{mtl_final_curve}, the model demonstrated rapid convergence within the first 25 epochs and maintained a stable validation error throughout the 200-epoch duration. \\
